    Project Management
    Project Management is the process of initiation, planning, execution, monitoring and closure. It is a team working together to accomplish goals within the specified time. Project Management in simple terms, mainly involves expertise within the fields of: what to do, when to try, the way to do and within what budget. Project Management deals with a project in 6 different phases, also referred to as the project life cycle. Now, let’s look at the 6 phases - Initiation Initiation is the first step for checking the feasibility of the project and measuring its worth. Planning The Planning phase involves making a blueprint for achieving the project’s objectives. Execution The Execution phase involves the allocation of resources and making of the deliverables. Monitoring The Monitoring phase involves the project manager in monitoring and controlling the working of the project by the team. Closure The closure is at the end of the project, where everything including what went wrong and what went right during the method is summed up. Good project management will result in achieving the specified result especially if there are tons of challenges and changes a corporation goes through. Everyone can keep track of what's happening and that they can manage to satisfy deadlines and budgets. There are a spread of characteristics or "skills" that are essential within the practice of Project Management. Outlined below are a number of the talents every effective Project Manager should possess within their utility belt: Leadership Skills - When leading a project you must be able to clearly convey the vision, the project goals and the overall objectives of the organization. Scope Management Skills - Scope Management is a practice which describes the processes required to ensure that the project includes all the work required, and only the work required, to complete the project successfully. The management portion is performed during the project initiation phase, where you'd outline and plan the scope, create the scope definition, scope verification, and scope change control. Relationships Building - Just like in your lives, trust and respect are two of the most important foundations for any relationship. Creating these building blocks are key traits of a leader to possess. Teams won't perform unless they trust the project manager and therefore the organization, and feel they're trusted. Trust will encourage people to propose ideas, suggest ways to reinforce work, speak of their concerns about the project and provide solid honest advice. Excellent "Active" Listening Skills - It is essential to understand the scope of your project. A Project Manager (PM) needs to have the capacity to listen to the needs of the person or organization. A PM's objective is to deliver the final product or service to the satisfaction of the owner also known as the "stakeholder" or "owner." Time Management Skills - As all projects are temporary, this is often a crucial skill within the practice of Project Management to manage some time effectively. Every project consists of tasks, deliverables, roles, and responsibilities. A Project Manager is usually playing a balancing act between the infamous triangle consisting of Scope, Time, and Cost. Risk Management Skills - There is risk with every project but within Project Management, managing risk is vital to any successful project. The fundamental definition of risk management is the identification, assessment, and prioritization of risks followed by coordinated and economical application of resources to minimize, monitor, and control the probability and/or impact of unfortunate events or to maximise the belief of opportunities. The word ‘engineering’ is sometimes described in more detail, especially in Great Britain, such as the manufacture or assembly of engines, machine tools, and machine parts. Engineers design, develop, modify, install, test and maintain a wide range of products and systems. They also provide consultation services and teach engineering courses at colleges and universities. The engineering field is divided into a number of specialized areas: 1.Mechanical engineering involves the design, production, inspection and repair of machinery, equipment and building materials as well as control systems and equipment to assess their condition and performance. These include automotive, construction and farm equipment, industrial installations and a variety of tools and equipment. 2.Electrical engineering involves the design, manufacturing, controlling, monitoring and testing of electrical and electronic equipments, machineries and systems. These systems vary from microscopic circles to national power generation and transmission systems. 3.Civil engineering involves the construction, construction, maintenance and inspection of major infrastructure projects such as highways, railways, bridges, tunnels, dams and airports. 4.Aviation engineering involves the design, production and testing of aircraft and aircraft design and components and materials such as aerospace, power plants, control and control systems, electrical and electronic systems, and communications and transmission systems. 5.Nuclear engineering involves the design, production, construction, operation and testing of equipments, systems. It also involves the processes in production, control and detection of nuclear radiation. 6.Civil engineering involves designing, constructing and inspecting load-bearing buildings with those commercial buildings, bridges and industrial infrastructure. 7.Biomedical engineering is the practice of designing systems, equipment and tools used to perform medical work. It involves extensive collaboration with physicians, including physicians, nurses, specialists, therapists and investigators, in order to identify, understand and meet their needs for systems, equipment and devices. 8.Chemical engineering is the practice of designing machinery, systems and processes for cleaning raw materials and mixing, adding chemicals and the use of chemicals to make important products. 9.Computer engineering is the practice of designing computer components, computers, networks and computer software. 10.Industrial engineering is the practice of designing and building facilities, machinery, systems and processes, processing of materials, and any number of other workplaces. 11.Environmental engineering is the practice of preventing, reducing and eliminating pollutants that affect air, water and land. It also includes finding and measuring land pollution levels, cleaning and rehabilitating polluted sites and ensuring compliance with local, state and financial policies.
